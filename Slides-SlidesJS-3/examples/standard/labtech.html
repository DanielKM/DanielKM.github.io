Wilfrid Laurier University

I started working as a lab technician with Wilfrid Laurier University in my third year of my undergraduate degree. I planned and executed an ambitious field study in the Northwest Territories. My field team constructed a base camp in the subarctic boreal forest where we stayed for two and a half months. The purpose of the expedition was to construct a Forest Global Earth Observatory forest dynamics plot. This was done in collaboration with the Smithsonian Institute as a long term study of the world's forests. My focus on the team was to operate the DGPS system and to delineate boundaries of the plot. The plot was 20 hectares in area through bogs, fens, and marshes.

After the trip to the NWT, I worked for a wetland ecology laboratory to delineate a species at risk (SAR) plant called Sida hermaphrodita (Virginia Mallow). This population was in Taquanyah Conservation Area, and was in direct competition with Phragmites australis. The study was done to determine why the Virginia Mallow was so successful despite being in close proximity to Phragmites plants. The mapping was done using an ArcPad Blue hand-held device in the field and ArcMaps in the lab.

After my master's degree was completed, I performed soil nutrient analysis using Inductively Coupled Plasma Optical Emission Spectrometry (ICP-OES). The ICP burns plasma hotter than the sun (6000-10000K) which ionizes the acid-digested samples and quantifies the constituent elements by the intensity of the emissions.
